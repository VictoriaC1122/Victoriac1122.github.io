Core Research Identity

Legitimacy, interpretation, and market formation in emerging technologies

I study how emerging technologies evolve from uncertain ideas into legitimate and actionable market constructs. My work focuses on the role of strategic communication, technological framing, and institutional context in shaping how innovation is evaluated by investors, organizations, and broader ecosystems. I am particularly interested in how firms navigate ambiguity and construct credibility in environments where performance signals are weak or unavailable.

Research Overview

How technological meaning becomes market-relevant

My research explores how firms communicate and position emerging technologies under conditions of uncertainty.

I examine how different forms of technological articulation influence external evaluation processes, particularly in early-stage markets where traditional performance indicators are absent.

Rather than treating technological narratives as neutral information, I conceptualize them as strategic signals embedded within broader evaluative environments. My work investigates when such signals enhance interpretability and credibility, and when they instead contribute to ambiguity or noise.

Across projects, I focus on the interaction between technological maturity, institutional expectations, and audience interpretation. This perspective allows me to better understand how innovation is translated into market-relevant meaning, and how firms shape the conditions under which new technologies become accepted and adopted.

Selected Research

Research on technology disclosure, legitimacy, and adoption under uncertainty

Current projects examine how firms make emerging technologies interpretable to external audiences and how credibility is constructed before market consensus has fully formed.

Selected Research 01

Signal or Noise? Technology Disclosure and Startup Funding

This project examines how early-stage technology ventures communicate technological capabilities to external audiences. It focuses on how different forms of technological articulation influence whether firms are perceived as credible and investable under conditions of uncertainty.

The study highlights how the informational value of technological narratives depends on their contextual interpretation rather than their mere presence.

Selected Research 02

Legitimacy-First Innovation

This research investigates how firms developing emerging technologies navigate environments characterized by institutional ambiguity and regulatory uncertainty. It proposes that, in such contexts, legitimacy is not a byproduct of innovation but a prerequisite for it.

The study develops a framework for understanding how firms align with institutional expectations, build credibility across stakeholders, and shape pathways toward mainstream adoption.
